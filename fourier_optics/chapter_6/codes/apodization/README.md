 Objective
Demonstrate how inverse apodization modifies the imaging performance of an optical system.
Theory
•	The pupil function defines the amplitude transmission of the aperture. 
•	The Fourier transform of the pupil gives the amplitude PSF. 
•	The intensity PSF is the squared magnitude of the field. 
•	The MTF is the Fourier transform of the PSF and describes the spatial frequency response. 
Expected Results
•	The inverse-apodized pupil concentrates transmission near the aperture edges. 
•	The PSF exhibits a narrower central lobe, indicating improved nominal resolution. 
•	The sidelobes become significantly larger, producing ringing artifacts. 
•	The MTF retains higher-frequency components compared to the normal pupil. 
Key Takeaway
Inverse apodization demonstrates a fundamental trade-off in Fourier optics: enhancing high spatial frequencies can improve apparent resolution, but this always comes at the cost of increased sidelobes and reduced image contrast. This example provides an intuitive introduction to super-resolution techniques based on pupil engineering.
